Landing pages come in lots of different sizes, styles, and designs. Clients can hire you to write anything from a simple opt-in page to a longer, more in-depth page.
They might call it a "squeeze page", "lead capture page", "lander", or even a "destination page".
Don’t get hung up on any of these details. Regardless of the size, shape, or what you call it, the job of a landing page is to promote a specific offer.
And the goal of your landing page copy is to get as many people as possible to take that offer.
In this lesson, I’ll show you exactly how to do that -- in any situation.
No matter what type of page you’re writing, the lessons you learn in this module will cover everything you need to create a high-converting page your clients will love.

The first step to writing a great landing page

Unlike other forms of copywriting (e.g. email), landing page copy doesn’t just sit there on the page. It fits into a design scheme, like furniture fits into a house.
Understanding this allows you to write better copy, faster, with less effort.

Think about it for a moment before moving on.

But the real answer is: Do you have a particular design or template in mind for this landing page?

Strategy Breakdown #1

You already know the importance of understanding the reader’s story before you write a word of copy. But it’s even more critical to writing landing page copy, because the strategic decisions you’ll make often have just as much to do with how the reader arrives on the page as they do with the offer itself!

Watch the video below to find out more:

Here was the reader’s story in this case:

  1. They decided they wanted to buy a house
  2. Next, they visited a real estate agent -- but didn’t know their credit score or price range
  3. The real estate agent sent them a link to this landing page where they could get a "Home Financing Certificate" that would show their ideal price range for a new home based on their credit history

Thinking about the landing page as an extension of where they came from, I knew the copy we opened with should feel like a continuation of their conversation with their real estate agent.

But I also didn’t want it to sound too formal or "businesslike". Can you figure out why? Take a moment to think about it before you keep reading.

It’s because buying a house is fun and exciting! Trying to hook them with words like "credit" or "home financing certificate" doesn’t sound like much fun, does it?

In fact, one of the first lines of copy I came up with for the page said that buying a home "should be exciting, not confusing." (This type of "why" oriented copy connects with people on a deep emotional level.)

Once I had their attention, it was time to explain how it works. I did this for two reasons:

  1. The template the client had picked out for this landing page contained a step-by-step "How it works" section
  2. Unless you’re offering something self-explanatory (e.g. an ebook), it’s usually a good idea to explain how it works

But notice that I didn’t just explain how it works in a cold, technical fashion.

I also explained what was in it for THEM (for example, a quicker approval process). People are always wondering, "What’s in it for ME?" -- the better you answer that question throughout your landing page copy, the more successful it’ll be.

Next, I included a testimonial -- again, this was part of the landing page template the client had chosen, but I always try to use testimonials on landing pages whenever possible. (You can get these right from the client, or you can charge them extra to interview their customers, as you learned in Module 1.)

The landing page template the client had chosen also contained some additional space at the bottom to mention (and briefly discuss) more features and benefits. I’d already loaded up the copy with plenty of these already, but this was a great place to mention an ancillary feature (free credit monitoring) that didn’t make sense to discuss earlier on the page.

I also used this space to reiterate that the offer was free, since it never hurts to mention key words like "free" multiple times.

For the remainder of the copy, I decided to "overcome objections." After my initial conversation with the client (and doing my own mental Role Reversal exercise), I knew the reader would likely have questions like:

  • "How do I know this is legit?"
  • And: "Will it hurt my credit score?"

So I made sure to devote a few lines of copy to addressing those concerns.

Had this landing page been aimed at "cold" traffic -- rather than readers who’d be warmed up by their relationship with their real estate agent -- I may have tried to overcome one or more of these objections even earlier, possibly even in the headline.

I also probably would have made the headline longer, explaining the offer in a bit more detail. But since visitors were sent here by a real estate professional, none of that was necessary.

One more thing you may have noticed is how authentic the copy feels. In fact, it almost seems as though I have an intimate knowledge of what it means to get a home financing certificate from this company -- even though I’d never heard of anything like this before the client asked me to write the page.

Strategy breakdown #2

While the target audience for the previous landing page was homebuyers, this page targeted lawyers. And not just any lawyers -- these would be attorneys looking for expert witnesses they could put on the witness stand to testify in court.

And unlike the previous page, the traffic arriving at this page was completely cold . The reader probably searched Google for a phrase like "expert witness", and had little-to-no knowledge about the offer they were about to see.

In other words, they’d be less familiar with what we were offering -- while also being more skeptical.

This meant we’d need to do more explaining and more convincing -- hence the longer page with more information to help them make a decision.

Let’s look at another key decision that differed from the previous example: The tone of the copy.

While buying a home is fun and exciting, hiring an expert witness to testify in court is serious business. In fact, while I sometimes discourage clients from being overly formal, in this case I specifically asked the client for a few technical terms we could throw around in order to build trust and credibility with this "hardcore" audience.

Let’s move on and talk about the copy itself.

At first glance, it might seem like the right way to tackle a page like this would be to focus on the benefits of finding a great expert witness (for example, winning more trials).

But when the client filled me in on the reader’s story, I started to realize it shouldn’t be the #1 focus of the page.

He explained that lawyers already understand the enormous value of using expert witnesses in court. Their big problem is FINDING one.

As the client explained to me, the right expert witness needs to have lots of specialized qualities -- including the right credentials, previous experience testifying in court, and more. This makes it extremely difficult to find the right expert witness, especially on a tight timeline.

And the few services that have historically tried to solve this problem didn’t do it very well. So lawyers were used to being burned -- paying too much for questionable "experts" from fast talking online marketers.

In order to convert as many readers as possible, I knew my top priority would be to show them that the service was reliable and trustworthy, and would deliver exactly what they need.

One framework I use in this type of situation is to ask the client, "If I were the reader, what are all of the things that might PREVENT me from signing up for this service?" Then I make sure we address each and every one of those things in the copy.

In other words, I treat the copy as a way to overcome every conceivable objection. Which is exactly what I did here. For example, the client explained to me that lawyers who use their competitors’ services end up getting billed twice -- once from the service, and once from the witness. Once I knew that, overcoming this objection was as easy as saying "You’ll only receive 1 bill from us -- period."

Another subtle concern the client pointed out to me was the fact that lawyers are often in a rush, and need to find an expert witness quickly. They’re worried about wasting valuable time on a "wild goose chase." So I made it a special point to mention that we’d respond to them quickly after they requested their quote.

There are tons of other great nuggets I got from the client -- all of which I never would have been able to come up with on my own. These are the type of details that will help you take your copy from "OK" to "awesome."

Strategy breakdown #3

These days, many businesses and professionals use landing pages as their website’s "home page" as a way to increase leads and sales, while giving their website a cleaner look.

While all landing pages contain some of the same elements -- like headline, bullets, call to action -- you can see that this one is quite different from the previous two examples I’ve shown you.

For one thing, it’s much shorter than the "expert witness" page we just looked at. Why do you suppose that is? The answer might surprise you…

The reason I kept this page short is because no matter how hard I pressed, the client did not give me as much information as I would have liked. And that’s OK! It’s just one of the realities of being a freelance copywriter.

Remember, you’re a writer, not a magician. The client was still perfectly happy with the copy, and even turned into a repeat customer.

Another thing you may notice is that this page starts off with a few longer paragraphs of copy.

Why?

As the client explained to me, readers arriving at this page are struggling with life and are often in a lot of pain.

They’re looking not just for a "solution," but for a human being who understands them.

If readers arrive and see a lot of sections filled with bullet points and one-liners, they wouldn’t feel that human touch you’d expect to find on a page like this.

In other words, this page wasn’t as much about "selling" as it was a personal message from a therapist to the people who needed her help.

I needed to make them feel like a real person was speaking to them, so I created longer paragraphs that are personal, conversational and human.

Similarly, you may have noticed that in the earlier example, the first call-to-action button is in the header directly beneath the headline. That makes sense for a "strictly business" type of service where many people will be ready to take the next step very quickly. But in this case, it would have been tacky and insensitive to move people to the appointment too quickly.

This is yet another reason why I’m not fond of "formulas" when it comes to writing great landing page copy. Yes, there are certain elements that every landing page will contain -- and we’ll discuss these in more detail shortly.

But never lose sight of the fact that each project has its own unique variables and goals. That’s how you move from being "just a writer" to a top professional clients love doing business with, over and over again.

As you can see, when it comes to landing page copy, there is no perfect "one size fits all" strategy. The key is to look at the specific offer, as well as the reader’s story, and think STRATEGICALLY.
It’s also a good idea to (briefly) explain your strategy to the client. Clients love this because it gives them context for the copywriting decisions and techniques you’re using.
There’s no need to go overboard -- a few bullet points of explanation can be a great way to warm them up and ensure they love the copy you’ve written for them.

The "heart and soul" of your landing page

If strategy is the engine that drives your landing page copy, amazing bullet points are the lifeblood that flows through it.
There are three different levels of bullet points you can choose from:
  • One-line "zinger" bullets
  • Expanded bullets
  • "Supercharged" mini-section bullets
Depending on the landing page you’re writing, you may choose to use one, two, or even a combination of all three.
Let’s go over each one in more detail:

1) One-line "zinger" bullets

These work best when you want to describe something that’s straightforward and direct. There are a few different ways you can use one-line "zinger" bullets, depending on the situation.
For example, you can use bullets to create curiosity. This works great for information-related offers, like webinars or ebooks. Here’s an example of the curiosity-building bullets on our FTW landing page offering my 5 Upwork hacks:
To write these bullets, I went through each email hack and asked myself two questions:
  1. "If I were reading these for the first time, which parts would I find most surprising?"
  2. "How can I make these sound as cool as possible?" (e.g. "Scientifically proven", "Raise my rates by $50/hr overnight", "Takes just 3 minutes to execute")
Then I described what the hack can help you accomplish, without mentioning the actual hack. BOOM! A short, irresistible set of bullets that makes it practically impossible to not sign up.
You can also use one-line "zinger" bullets to highlight straightforward features, like this:

Or even to simply highlight the BENEFITS of an offer.
When I write "benefit-driven" bullets, the question I ask myself is not "What will they get out of this product?", but rather "What will they NOT get if they DON’T have this product?" This taps into their FOMO ("fear of missing out"), which is one of the most powerful psychological forces you can use.
Another great use for one-line "zinger" bullets is to call out specific readers by explaining exactly who an offer is for:
You can also call out specific readers by their DESIRES. This works great when you don’t have specific information, such as their profession, geographic location, gender, age, etc.
In the example above, I didn’t need to come up with any of these bullets on my own. They came directly from the client, who gave me a list of lessons that were covered in his information product. All I needed to do was choose the lessons that were most exciting, and tweak them into bullets that "zing".

2) Expanded bullets

These contain more copy than "zingers" and are usually preceded by a short title. They’re my "go-to" bullets when I need to convey detailed information, and a one-liner just won’t cut it.
For example, if you want to persuade readers to buy an ebook, it’s more powerful to spell out the contents of each chapter, rather than just mentioning the benefits.
You can also use expanded bullets to persuade readers to do something that asks them to make a commitment, such as:
  • Signing up for a free trial
  • Ordering a monthly subscription
  • Scheduling an appointment
  • Or requesting a demo
Or you can use them to break down and simplify ideas that are technical or confusing, like business services and software products.

Mini-section bullets are more of a paragraph than a bullet, and they are usually "roped off" into their own section on the page. Most mini-section bullets begin with a subheadline.
Supercharged mini-section bullets are a great way to pack in a lot of information, while keeping the page neat and "clean".
For example, if your landing page is promoting a financial advisory firm that offers a variety of services, you can use mini-section bullets to show readers a "snapshot" of each service.

Getting readers to "pull the trigger"

It may be tempting to think that once your copy has done its job persuading the reader, the rest is just logistics.
But the reality is, landing page readers are not committed until they’ve "pulled the trigger". Any snag in the process can give readers second thoughts, which can lead to fewer conversions.
For this reason, your call-to-action needs to be strategically crafted to get readers to cross the finish line.
I always make sure to tell clients that calls-to-action that have fewer fields to fill out are scientifically proven to get more signups.
Asking for less information doesn’t just make the process simpler. When readers see you asking for too much personal information (eg. address, phone number), their "antennas" kick into high gear, and they get ambivalent about the offer.

Most call-to-action sections have 2 copy areas:

The headline is not supposed to introduce any new information. It’s job is to give readers a final emotional "push" across the finish line. It should be short and exciting, and specify the action you want readers to take. You can use this area to summarize the offer, highlight a key benefit, or remind the reader the offer is free.

Writing an enticing headline -- in under 10 minutes

A lot of copywriters spend a long time coming up with their landing page headline. It never takes me more than a few minutes to come up with several powerful headlines.
Here are the 3 steps I use to come up with multiple headlines for any landing page:

1) Copy and paste

My "first line of defense" with headline writing is to see if it’s already written. After you’ve written the copy, you can often one or even multiple lines that can be used as your headline -- either "as is", or with a bit of tweaking. It may be a bullet, subhead, or any other part of the page.
Before I write a headline from scratch, I go down the page looking for any line that has the potential to be a headline. Then I see how it looks, and if needed, I tweak it. Presto -- I’ve got an instant headline.
Here’s an example of a line I originally placed near the bottom of a piece of landing page copy -- promoting the service of a high-end logo designer -- which ended up being my headline:
"This Is One Of The Most Powerful -- Yet Underrated -- Marketing Tools In Your Arsenal"

The headline I wrote in 10 seconds

I was once hired to write a landing page for a company that produced state-of-the-art hearing aids.

My client explained that the number one benefit his hearing aid offered was that the sound was super clear and lifelike, while his competitors’ hearing aids sounded metallic and distorted. His product imitated the human ear so closely, it amplified the sounds people wanted to hear, and filtered out background noise, solving one of the biggest problems faced by people who are hearing-impaired.

Because many companies make similar promises (eg. "sounds lifelike"), but don’t deliver, I needed a headline that conveyed that this life-changing product was the next best thing to having a fully-operational human ear.

I came up with several options that were "OK", before I noticed a line buried in my own landing page copy --

"Our hearing aids ask themselves ‘What would an ear do?’…and they do the EXACT same thing!"

I instantly realized that my headline had been staring me in the face! The client loved it, and told me that he was so excited, he wouldn’t be able to sleep that night.

2) Use my headline generator

If you can’t find any line in your copy that works, your next line of defense is to use my headline generator tool (in the bonus section) to quickly create a headline based on proven templates.

3) Write 25 headlines

If all else fails, write 25 headlines as quickly as possible. (This is similar to the subject line technique we discussed in Module 2.) With practice, you should be able to do this in 5-10 minutes max. Go through each one and eliminate the ones you don’t like. You should be left with at least 3 or 4 solid headlines.

Three important rules to keep in mind when writing headline copy:

1) Avoid headline confusion.

Remember, the headline is NOT the first thing the reader is seeing. The landing page is not the first stop on their journey, and they’ve come here by clicking on a link. To have a successful headline, it needs to keep the flow moving, and be a smooth progression.
When writing your headline, make sure to keep in mind the advertisement, email, blog post, or link the reader is coming from, and figure out what they’re expecting to see next.

2) Eliminate extra words.

You can almost always delete these extraneous words from your headline: Just, so, very, really, it, and, but, of, some, and like.

3) Send a few options.

It’s always a good idea to send 2 or 3 additional headline options to your client. Make it clear which one you like the best, and label the others "alternate options."
Headlines are more art than science, and it’s always helpful to give your client a few to choose from. Extra headlines also come in handy in case your client wants to do an A/B test.

Module 6 Recap

  • Landing page copy is an “extension” of the copy the reader sees before they get there.
  • Always ask the client if they have a template picked out for the landing page you’re working on.
  • To write like an expert about any offer, you should personally try the offer — just like a reader would.
  • Bullet points are the “heart and soul” of a great landing page.
  • There are 3 types of bullets: One-line “zingers”, expanded bullets, and mini-sections.
  • Use zingers to build curiosity, and highlight features and benefits.
  • Use expanded bullets when you need to describe features/benefits in more detail.
  • Use mini-sections to highlight and explain your most attractive features (or overcome common objections).
  • Your call-to-action should give readers one final emotional “push” across the finish line.
  • Don’t try to stuff too much information into your call-to-action — keep it short and specific.
  • You can jazz up your CTA by: Showing readers it’s fast and easy to sign up (“Get a quote in 5 minutes); Writing it from the reader’s perspective (“Raise my IQ”); Adding urgency (“Join now — space is limited”).
  • To write a great headline: 1) See if you can find one “hidden” in your copy; 2) Use my landing page headline generator tool; 3) Write 25 headline options as quickly as possible.
  • Avoid “headline confusion” by considering the copy the reader has seen before landing on the page.

Bonus: How to "juice up" bullet points for maximum effect

One line "zinger" bullets may be short, but in the hands of a properly skilled copywriter they can pack a serious punch. With a little extra effort, you can juice up your bullets to be 10x more powerful and tap into readers’ deepest emotions.

The first key is to use words that spark concrete, vivid mental images in the mind of the reader.

Here’s an example of some one-liner bullets one FTW student wrote for a "brain-boosting" health supplement. Many competing products promise to help boost "energy" and "focus", but he came up with a fresh way to present these benefits.  

According to the latest research, your brain can handle ANYTHING-- as long as you pump it with the right fuel.

[PRODUCT NAME] gives you:

  • Sharp, piercing focus--like a rigorous aerobic workout
  • The energy burst of a Venti Latte
  • Calm, soothing pleasure of a hot jacuzzi
  • The powerful reinvigoration of a long, deep sleep
  • Mood-enhancing effect of a majestic ocean sunrise

Do you see how he tapped into the reader’s imagination and created excitement by using descriptive words and visual imagery?

You can also tap into the reader’s emotions by describing real-life scenarios:

  • Never again suffer the embarrassment of your child throwing a tantrum at the mall while people gawk and judge your parenting ability

If your client is sparse on information, and you need to create extra bullets, you can often squeeze 2 bullets out of one single idea, like this…

Example bullet point:

  • Easy-to-learn technique to make your child happy and calm, even when they seem out-of-control

The same bullet, split into 2:

  • Easy-to-learn technique
  • Make your child happy and calm, even when they seem out-of-control

You can also get more mileage out of a single bullet point by "flipping" it from a negative to a positive viewpoint (and vice versa):

Example bullet point:

  • • Learn how to pick winning stocks that bring in a return of 5X or even 10X

The same bullet, "flipped" to the negative:

  • Don’t lose money by investing in the wrong stocks

Remember, the most powerful bullets create CONCRETE mental images in the minds of readers. After you’ve written your bullets, scan your first draft for anything that sounds vague, abstract, or overly broad or general. Then bring it down to earth by making it more specific, concrete, and visual.

Example bullet points (vague/abstract):

  • Every recipe is healthy, pure, and natural
  • This software will quickly streamline your business and boost sales
  • Learn how to raise children successfully

The same bullets, "juiced up":

  • Every recipe is 100% vegan, gluten free, dairy-free, and refined sugar free
  • This software will cut your employee hours by 25%, and 3X your monthly revenue...in just 6 weeks
  • Learn how to get your child to follow instructions and treat people with respect


Bonus: Never-get-stuck ingredient list for landing pages

Feeling stuck on your landing page copy, or just unsure of what to write next? Here are a few of my favorite “ingredients” you can use to get over the hump. Just go through this list, pick the one you think would fit best, and bam -- you’re back in business.

Intro copy. A short introduction can help readers move seamlessly from your headline into the details of your offer. There are many different ways to do this -- you can mention the problem you’re going to help them overcome, give them a brief overview of the solution, or even mention the “why” behind the solution. You can even combine all 3 of these!

“Buying a home should be exciting, not confusing. This new program empowers you to make informed decisions by giving you instant, individualized answers to common questions like, “What’s my ideal price range?” and “What’s my credit score?”

Create urgency. It’s never a bad idea to give readers extra motivation to act. Urgency, or any other type of scarcity are great ways to do that. Is a sale ending soon? Will prices be going up? Are there a limited number of products, free consults, or webinar seats? All of these are great examples of how to use strategic urgency to enhance your landing page copy.

You can also get creative! For example, I once paid $1,500 for one of James Altucher’s evergreen products after he said the “price would probably go up soon.”

Write a bulleted list. You could write bullets about a lead magnet, a product or service, a list of features and benefits, a webinar, or anything else. I even use bullet points to “sell” readers on clicking through to a blog post or other article. One of my clients used to say that “great copywriters think in bullet points” -- a lesson that has stuck with me ever since.

Add testimonials. I always like to ask clients if they have any testimonials from people they’ve helped through their business. These can be great material for your landing page copy. You can use a single testimonial or you can even use an entire block of testimonials in order to build even more trust and credibility.

FAQ. A list of frequently asked questions and answers about a product or service is an excellent go-to piece for any landing page.

The client may have a FAQ list already, so be sure to ask. If they don’t, be prepared to help them come up with one (remember to charge them for your time if you do this).

“How it works”. If your landing page offer isn’t something that’s universally understood (like downloading an ebook or free report), it’s wise to consider spelling out exactly how it works for readers. Often, the best way to do that is to write it out in the form of a step by step process (“Step-1”, “Step-2”, etc).

Credibility markers. If your client has a well known customer or partner company, pointing that out can be a great way to boost their credibility with readers. Or if they’ve ever been featured on a popular site or podcast, mentioning that can achieve a similar effect. Even a simple “About Us” blurb can go a long way toward establishing trust and credibility, especially if you sprinkle in details like the number of customers they’ve served, and how many years they’ve been in business.

Bonus: A simple hack for boosting landing page conversions

Did you know that the layout your client uses for their landing page copy can affect the performance of your copy?

For example, having at least one Call To Action button “above the fold” (high enough on the page so the reader doesn’t have to scroll to find it) is a known technique for boosting conversions.

As a freelance copywriter, you’re obviously not responsible for landing page design. But you can still use this knowledge to your advantage, making sure clients view you as a trusted advisor and a true expert with a depth of knowledge that can help them improve their results.

Another piece of advice I always give landing page clients is that their Call To Action buttons shouldn’t match the page’s color scheme too closely, since contrasting colors tend to convert better. (For example, if a landing page is mostly green, an orange button will usually convert better than a green one that blends in and is easier to miss.)

Clients love this because it gives them tons of added value, much more than most freelance copywriters offer. It puts me into a “category of one” in their minds.

Another trick I use is to find high-converting templates for clients to use. You can find pre-existing landing page templates at LeadPages. net for just about any industry, product, or offer. 

For one thing, you can go to a site like LeadPages and look for inspiration from pre-existing templates that are designed for something similar to what your client is trying to accomplish.

For example, let’s say one of my clients wanted landing page copy to get people to sign up for a free ebook. If they didn’t have a design template or layout decided on yet, I’d go to LeadPages and look for a specific template that was designed specifically for this client’s goals.

Bonus: Great questions for landing page clients

  • What is the goal of this landing page? (Examples: To get people to register for a free 1-hour webinar, buy a $9.95 ebook, sign up for a free trial of software, etc.)
  • Who is the target audience for this offer? Please be as detailed as possible -- don’t just say “entrepreneurs” or “parents of kids who are heading off to college” -- tell me their STORY. (The more I know about them, the better the copy will be.) • Please tell me all of the different ways a reader might get to this page. (Examples: They did a Google search for “______”, clicked a link in an email, responded to a Facebook ad, etc.)
  • Please send me any copy/ads/emails that a reader would see BEFORE they get to this landing page.
  • Please send me all of the written information you have about the product/service being offered on the landing page. If it’s an information product, please send me the product itself so I can review it. If you can’t send me the whole product -- for example, if it’s a webinar that hasn’t taken place yet -- please send me as much information as you can (e.g. an outline, PowerPoint deck, etc).
  • What burning desire or problem does this offer solve for the reader? Please be as detailed as possible and tell me as much as you know about the reader’s story, including their mindset, their emotions, and anything else that plays into WHY they would want this offer.
  • Please tell me about all of the features and benefits the reader will get from this offer. (Keep in mind that features and benefits are two different things: The octagonal shape of a pencil is a feature; the benefit is that it won’t roll off the table.) • Imagine I’m a member of your target audience who is reading this landing page once it’s complete. What are all of the questions/concerns/objections I might have that could PREVENT me from signing up for this offer? (Please be as specific as possible.)
  • Are there any “credibility markers” we could use to build trust and increase conversions for this page? These can be names of clients you’ve done business with, publications you’ve been featured or quoted in, or even facts or statistics that boost your credibility (e.g. “2,500 happy customers”). It can even be something as simple as an “About Us” blurb that connects with readers.
  • Is there a specific landing page that you think is really powerful? If so, please paste a link to it below. (It does not need to be related to your product or industry.)
  • Is there a particular tone you think will be most effective for your target audience? (Examples include: Conversational, formal, professional, authoritative, fun and exciting.)
  • Are there certain words or phrases that should definitely appear on this page?
  • Are there any words or phrases you’d like to AVOID using?
  • What is the call to action for this landing page? (In other words, what do you want readers to DO after they read it?)
