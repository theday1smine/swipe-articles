An explainer video is a short animated video designed to get viewers to take some sort of ACTION. It could be buying a product, booking an appointment, downloading a white paper, calling for a free consult, or anything else.

Explainer videos can go by many different names, including whiteboard video, animated video, homepage video, marketing video, and more.

Don’t get too hung up on the terminology or format… If someone wants a short animated video, you’ll be able to use what you learn in this module to help them.

Writing explainer videos comes with a lot of benefits :

  • As online video production gets easier and cheaper, the demand for great scripts just keeps growing
  • They’re fun! You get to be creative and tell entertaining stories (even if you’re not a “natural storyteller”)
  • You can charge good money for them (it’s not uncommon to get paid $150+ or more, even for a short script of just 150 words)

In this module, you’ll learn exactly how to write an effective explainer video script for any situation.

Your real job as an explainer video copywriter  

Years ago, explainer videos were used almost exclusively by businesses as a quick, easy way to break down complicated, confusing, or technical products or ideas.

But as explainer videos have skyrocketed in popularity, it’s become fashionable for any business to have an explainer video — even if they don’t have anything new, complicated, or unfamiliar to actually explain. 

Today organizations big and small use them to market anything from food products, to muffler repair, dentistry, training courses, live events, charities, and much much more. Like this one — from a financial planner:

Regardless what type of business (or nonprofit) you’re writing for, a great explainer video is more like a mini-movie than an instruction manual. Your primary job is to connect with viewers on an emotional level. 

Yes, your video scripts can and will explain your client’s product or service. But it’s the emotional triggers that grab viewers’ interest, keep them engaged, and even get them to act.

This idea of tapping into emotions — rather than relying on logic — is something you’ll find useful throughout all forms of copywriting. And it has special importance to explainer videos. 

To understand why, think about how you feel when you start playing an online video: 

Yes, you probably want to learn something, but you also expect to be entertained!

You don’t need to be the second coming of Chris Rock in order to pull this off when you’re writing an explainer video script. I’ll show you how anyone can do it.

Let’s discuss the first step…

How to structure your script

Structuring your script properly is the best way to set yourself up for success, before you even write a single word.

It’s like having a recipe when you sit down to cook — it makes things easier and ensures you’ll end up with a delicious meal.

There are 6 main “ingredients” you’ll usually be working with when you write an explainer video script:

Introduce the problem

The first part of every explainer video. You simply create a character and give him/her a problem they need to solve.

Expand the problem

This is where you “raise the stakes” for your character. You can make the problem more “real” by going into more detail; pile on additional “second order” problems; or even talk about all the bad stuff that will happen if the character fails to solve the problem.

Introduce the solution

Now it’s time to give some hope to your character — as well as some love to your client — by introducing their product/service into the mix. This is the solution that will ultimately help your character solve their problem and give him or her a happy ending.

Explain the solution in more detail

This is why they call it an explainer video; here you’ll elaborate on the solution so viewers understand exactly how it works, how it can help them solve THEIR problem in real life.

Overcome an objection

This will typically be the #1 objection or frequently asked question people have about the product/service you’re showcasing in the video. (This is the least common of all the ingredients, but it can come in handy when you need it.)

Call to action

This is where you tell viewers what to do next. It can be checking out a link, downloading a white paper, calling for more information, or anything else your client wants viewers to do after watching the video.

You won’t always use all of these ingredients. Which ones you decide to use, and how much space you devote to each one, will depend upon a number of variables, including:

  • The purpose of the video (a simple product like a potato peeler won’t require as much explanation as a new tech device)
  • The length of the video
  • How much information the client gives you (ideally clients will give you plenty of information for all 6 areas — but in reality, sometimes you just need to work with what you’ve got)

3 Rules for writing awesome explainer video copy

Rule #1: People first, products second

People can’t relate to products or services — at least not directly.

But they relate incredibly well to other people.

That’s why Kim Kardashian can instantly turn an ordinary product into a hot new trend with a single tweet:

Of course, your scripts aren’t likely to feature many celebrities. But they will feature another one of your viewer’s favorite VIPs: THEMSELVES!

No, they won’t literally see themselves featured in your video… Instead, you’ll create a character — like Joe, or Jane — and give them all of the same problems, challenges, fears, reservations, hopes, and dreams as the person watching the video.

This is also why I spend at least as much time asking the client about their CUSTOMER, as I do on their product or service.
As you know by now, asking the right questions is one of the keys to writing great copy that gets results. This is incredibly important in explainer video copywriting because you need to be able to tell the viewer’s story in an authentic way.
Let’s look at a real life example of how I do this:

Imagine someone planning for retirement watching this story unfold. They can’t help but relate to it…because it’s their story! The more the viewer sees themselves in the video, the more successful your script will be.

Rule #2: Show, don’t tell

In the climactic scene of the movie Glory, Colonel Robert Gould Shaw needs to inspire his soldiers to charge the confederate army — in spite of the fact that many of them will be walking into certain death.
Does he give a rousing speech? HELL NO. 
Instead, he points his sword forward…and begins the charge himself.

Colonel Shaw knew that the IMAGE of him charging forward would be infinitely more powerful than any combination of words he could muster together.
The world’s most elite copywriters have all internalized this same truth.
They don’t use words to DESCRIBE. 
They use them to SHOW you things. 
That’s how they connect to your deepest emotions and spur you to action.
To see what I mean, compare the following two sentences:
“The thought of an IRS audit made Laura feel stressed and anxious.”
“Laura imagined an IRS auditor knocking on her door. She massaged her temples as her head began to throb.”
Notice how the first one uses words to tell you what Laura is feeling… While the second one SHOWS you what she’s feeling, giving you a far more vivid and emotional EXPERIENCE.
Which one do you think would convert better? Personally, I’d bet $10,000 on “Door #2” without a second thought.

Start with the problem

A good explainer video script starts out by telling the viewer’s STORY — as authentically as possible. 

You don’t want or need to go into every detail of their life. The key is to understand their problem — whatever it is they need to accomplish — in a way that feels “real” to them.

At a minimum, after watching the opening of your video, the viewer needs to:

  • Know this is meant for them specifically
  • Identify with the character in the video (“Hey, that’s exactly what I’m dealing with right now!”)
  • Recognize that you have a solid understanding of exactly what they’re going through (this helps you position your client as an authority who can help the viewer solve the problem)

Next, Introduce the solution

Once you’ve done everything you can to showcase the problem the viewer wants to solve, it’s time to introduce — and explain — the solution.

This part of your script is going to be mainly about the client and the product or service they’re offering. 

You’ll explain exactly how it works, in a logical, step-by-step fashion. 

But make sure you don’t lose sight of the most important person: The viewer! 

You need to make sure you tie the solution back to THEIR needs. How it helps them solve THEIR problem. Let’s take a closer look at the “solution” portion of the first script I showed you at the beginning of this lesson:

Adjusting the length of your script

Unlike emails, press releases, and other types of copywriting, explainer video clients usually need their script to contain a specific number of words. That’s because they’ve generally already decided on how long their video should be — your job is to write the number of words that fits that predetermined length.

As a rule of thumb, 150 words equals about one minute of finished video. So if a client needs a 2-minute video, you’ll write 300 words…and so on. (There’s a little bit of wiggle room — you can usually squeeze in up to 175 words per minute if you need to, though it’s not ideal.)

Once you’ve written the first draft of your script, your next step is to check the word count to see how closely it matches up with what the client needs.

Most likely, you’ll either have too many words — or too few. This is perfectly normal, and I’ll show you my favorite quick fixes for dealing with either scenario.

If you have too many words…

If your client gives you a lot of info to work with, or you’re writing about a complicated topic, your first draft may end up longer than expected. 

There are several ways to cut down your script when that happens. You may not need to do all of these — start with the least drastic ones first, and move down the list if you need to.

  1. Look for “dead weight” words you can safely delete. Just, so, very, really, it,and, but, of, some, and like can be removed surprisingly often.
  2. Look for sentences that are repetitive, redundant, or don’t add anything to your script. Are there 2 sentences that each say more or less the same thing? Cut one. Did you mention a fringe benefit that isn’t too important? Remove it — as well as any other “expendable” sentences you need to in order to hit your target word count.
  3. Wait 24 hours. Whenever possible, take a day off and come back with fresh eyes. You’ll be amazed at how much easier it is to spot editing opportunities after not looking at your script for one full day.
  4. If you must, you can always resort to “telling” instead of “showing.” It usually takes up fewer words. For example, you can replace “Ashley felt like she hadn’t slept in days…between getting the kids off to school by 8am, working 9-5, and keeping her house in order…” — with “Courtney is a stressed out mom.” It’s not ideal, but you’re a copywriter, not a miracle worker.
  5. Trim down the call-to-action. Again, this isn’t the ideal solution. But when you’re working with a strict word count, sometimes you need to do whatever it takes to get there — and that’s OK.
  6. See if the client is open to a longer script. When you believe the only way to cut your word count is to remove essential content, I suggest a quick email or phone call to the client. Let them know that you’re happy to cut it down if that’s what they want, but you think the longer version would be better. (You can always send them both options and let them decide.)

“But Danny, what if my script has too few words?”

If your first draft falls short of the target word count, no worries. There are plenty of ways to get it up to the right count, without sacrificing an ounce of quality. 

In fact, if you play your cards right you can even make your script better and longer at the same time.

The key is to start with lots of good information. You’ll find tons of juicy questions to ask explainer video clients in the bonus section — here’s how you can use it to beef up your script’s word count:

  1. Overcome an objection (or even 2). This is one of the quickest, easiest, and most valuable ways to add some length to your script. Even if your first draft already overcomes an objection, consider adding a second one. Go back to the info you got from your client and see what you can do.
  2. Teach something of value. People love to learn things that lead them closer to solving a burning problem or achieving an intense desire. One of my favorite explainer videos was from a divorce attorney — he advised viewers going through a divorce to keep away from social media since their posts could be used against them in court. This approach is especially useful when your client is struggling to differentiate themselves from their competitors. Just ask, “What are some surprising tips we can give?” Then use 1-3 of them to beef up your script.  
  3. Do a “final recap” or summary. Need to add a few more words to your script, but feel like you’ve run out of things to say? No problem, just recap what you’ve already said! Going over your main points again at the end of your script doesn’t just work as “filler” — it’s also a great way to refresh the viewer’s memory. (You can also recap the BENEFITS the viewer will get from using the solution.)
  4. Spruce up your Call To Action. Adding a few sentences to your CTA is one of the easiest ways to increase the value AND word count of your script. You can tie it to a benefit; expand a on how easy it is to take the next step; or even repeat the CTA again in different words. You can also add the company’s tagline if they have one.
  5. Add a testimonial. You can copy and paste it directly into your script, or edit it a bit if you need to (if you do edit it, make sure to get the client’s approval).

 

Module 4 recap

  • A great explainer video doesn’t just explain — it should also entertain and get viewers to take action.
  • There are 6 main “ingredients”: Introduce the problem, Expand the problem, Introduce the solution, Explain the solution, Overcome an objection, and Call To Action.
  • The best explainer videos feature characters with the same problems, challenges, fears, hopes, and dreams as the viewer.
  • Use “visual words” that SHOW the story unfolding, instead of just describing it.
  • The opening of your script should show viewers you understand THEIR SPECIFIC problem — in as much detail as possible.
  • You can amp up the drama of your script by raising the DEGREE of the problem, and adding CONTRAST to make it seem more stark.
  • Your explanation of how the solution works should be a logical, step-by-step “demonstration.
  • Make sure to tie the solution back to the viewer’s needs, and how it can make their life better.
  • Sometimes a deep technical explanation of how the solution works isn’t required. In that case, just show the viewer what they’ll get from using it.
  • When writing the Call To Action, don’t just tell the viewer what to do — tell them WHY they should do it.
  • You can beef up your Call To Action using “urgency,” or by calling out a specific group of people who should take the next step.
  • As a rule of thumb, 150 words is equal to about 1-minute of video.
  • If your first draft is too long, you can: Cut “dead weight” words, remove repetitive or redundant sentences, wait 24 hours and edit with fresh eyes. If needed, you can also resort to “telling” instead of “showing,” trim down the Call To Action, or even ask if the client is open to a longer script.
  • If your first draft is too short, you can: Teach something of value, overcome an an objection (or 2), add in a “recap”, spruce up your Call To Action, or include one or more testimonials.

Bonus: Great questions to ask explainer video clients

  • What is the purpose of this explainer video? What is the “solution” you are you promoting in it? (This can be a product or service, but it can also be a free solution, like a white paper, free consult, etc.)
  • What is the main problem or burning desire your viewer has (that your solution is intended to help them solve)? • Why are they seeking a solution to this NOW (as opposed to yesterday, last week, or last year)?
  • How do they think about that problem or burning desire? What does it feel like? What sort of words do they use to describe it or complain about it to a friend? If they woke up at 4am thinking about it, what would their internal dialogue sound like? • What will happen to them if they don’t solve the problem (or achieve the burning desire) soon? Will things get worse? Will a window of opportunity close? Please explain.
  • How will your solution help them fix their problem or achieve their burning desire? What are the exact, specific steps involved? Please answer in as much detail as possible.
  • What are ALL the ways their life be improved after using your solution? If they were talking to a friend or relative describing their successful experience, what would that conversation sound like?
  • Are there other methods they could use to solve their problem or achieving their burning desire? Have they likely tried any of these?
  • If they haven’t tried anything else, is there a reason why not? (Examples: “No solution existed before this one”; “They couldn’t afford the other solutions out there”; “They’ve been in denial, telling themselves the problem will just go away on its own”)
  • Is there anything that makes YOUR solution different or superior to the alternatives? Is it cheaper, better, more durable, more reliable, or…? Does it come with better customer service, does it help businesses become more profitable, is it better for the environment? Is it just for men, just for women, or does it have more bells and whistles? Please describe ANY important differences in as much DETAIL as possible.
  • Why are other solutions to this problem not as effective as yours? What shortcomings, problems, or frustrations will they encounter if they don’t choose your product?
  • How do your customers usually hear about you? Do friends send them to your website? Do they google something? Or…?
  • What EMOTIONS will viewers of this video associate with their problem or burning desire? These could include shame, fear, anger, jealousy of others who’ve achieved what they want to achieve, anxiety, or anything else.

(Note that I’m not looking for one-word answers like “shame,” but a SPECIFIC and DETAILED answer like “Jane is worried about how she might appear to her bosses if she tries to negotiate her salary. She’s afraid she’ll come across as overly aggressive, ungrateful, or even ‘unladylike’.”)

  • What are all the ways your solution will make the viewer’s life better after they use it?
  • In longer videos, sometimes there may be enough time to teach viewers something surprising, or give them some juicy tips. Readers love this!

    For example, a divorce attorney can advise viewers going through a divorce to refrain from using social media, where their words can be used against them in court. What are some surprising tips that viewers of this video might like to know about?

  • What are the most important features and benefits you’d like to highlight about your product? (Keep in mind the difference between features and benefits...for example, the octagonal shape of a pencil is a feature -- the benefit is that it won’t roll off your desk.)
  • What are the top OBJECTIONS or frequently asked questions customers have about the solution you’re offering? What would you say to overcome each of those objections?
    What is the core message of this video?
  • What is the call-to-action of this video -- what exactly do you want viewers to do after watching it?
  • How will people be finding this video? (eg. on a landing page, link sent in an email, Facebook ad etc.)
  • Are the people who see this video already aware of your company and the solution you’re offering?
    If you could sum up, in a few sentences or less, what you would like the viewer to say to himself after seeing this video, what would it be?
  • If possible, please describe the overall tone you feel would be most fitting for this video (upbeat, serious, fun, exciting, somber, etc):
  • Which, if any, best describe what you’re looking to accomplish with this video:
  1. Introduce a new product or idea
  2. Build more awareness about something people are already familiar with
  3. Show people why your product is better or different than others
  4. Get people excited about something they are already aware of
  5. Explain something complex or confusing, in a simple-to-understand way
  6. Teach people something
  • Can you give me links to any webpages or marketing material that describe the solution you’re offering? (This can include any relevant pages on your website)
  • Do you have any customer testimonials about your solution?
  • How long is this video going to be?
  • Is there a particular story you envision for this video? What’s happening in it? Who are the characters? Where does it take place?
  • Does your company have a slogan or tagline? How about the solution we’re promoting -- does it have a specific slogan or tagline attached to it?
  • Is there any way we can add urgency into the call to action of this video? For example, “Available for a limited time”, “Only a few slots available”, etc?
  • Are there any particular words or phrases you’d like to see included in this script? • Are there any words or phrases you’d like to AVOID saying?
 

 

Bonus: Case Study: Turning a client’s raw answers into a finished script

Even though explainer video scripts tend to be relatively short pieces of copy (usually just a few hundred words or less), getting great information from the client is extremely important.

Your script needs to be tight… There’s zero room for fluff or filler. You need to showcase the viewer’s problem and describe a specific solution -- and you need to do it in an authentic way. The more great information you start with, the easier it’ll be to pull it off.

With that said, the client doesn’t need to answer EVERY single one of your questions in amazing detail in order to give you enough info to work with.

In the following example, the client returned my questionnaire with lots of vague, skimpy responses. But that’s OK because a few of her responses were good enough to give me all of the material I needed to write a good script!

Check out her answers below:  

What type of story do you envision for this video?

A home environment - like a dining room. A 50 year old woman with a stack of papers or with her parent. Images representing bank accounts/banking, insurance accounts/agents, trying to sort through it all.

What is the problem your product/service solves?

There is a workbook that makes it easy to document all my (or my parent’s) financial and insurance information so that family can resolve my estate upon my death.

This simple workbook provides an easy way to record important financial and insurance information, as well as end of life preferences. At death, loved ones will be able to access the information needed with less time and stress.

What will happen to someone who doesn’t use your solution?

Family members will spend months digging for account and insurance information with mixed success, often placing stress on family relationships.

What would you like the call to action at the end of this video to be? (What action do you want viewers to take?)

Don’t leave a legacy full of unanswered questions. -----.com helps you plan for your loved ones tomorrow -- go to www.---- .com to learn more (buy the workbook)

If you look closely, she gave me all the STORY details I needed in order to put the pieces together. In just a few minutes I was able to write the following draft:
