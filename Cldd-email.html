PART I

Before you write your first email

 

Master the game

To win the game, you need to know what game you are playing.

 

Interestingly enough, most advice on outbound email focuses primarily on the writing aspect. You will get plenty of advice in this book on how to approach the writing part, but it’s really just one piece of the puzzle.

 

To get all of the pieces of the puzzle together and really grow your business with outbound cold emails, there are a few more things you need to master first.

 

What no one will tell you is that the game is either won or lost before you even send your first email.

 

In this section, I’m going to show you what you need to know and look for so that you can be successful at it, while at the same time minimizing the negative impact of failure on you and your business.

 

 

 

 

 

 

CHAPTER ONE

The most important metric you have to know

 

Sender Reputation

Because cold email can be a very cheap way to acquire new customers, it's very often abused. As a result, a lot of different techniques are used by email services (receiving but also sending) to quickly determine if emails sent are spam or not.

 

Everyone knows (or should know) that adding “Viagra” or “cheap loan” to your subject line won't do you any good and will be intercepted by most spam filters. But there is a much more problematic and persistent criteria that you need to know about. Why? Because it could kill your business over time.

 

It can affect every email you send from your company domain name and can take weeks — if not months — to fix. In fact, it can be so annoying that some companies have gone so far as to migrate to new domain names just to solve the problem. This is especially important to know when you begin cold emails because you may not recognize the problem until it’s too late.

 

I’m talking about sender reputation. It’s a simple number that gets assigned to you and can vary from 0 (bad) to 100 (good).

 

It’s important because while a good reputation will get you almost straight to the inbox, a bad one almost certainly guarantees your email will land in the spam folder. It’s a strong factor to watch for good deliverability, but it gets worse. Because the reputation is actually on the domain, creating new emails won’t actually help you get out of this. In fact, once your sender reputation is damaged, it's very hard to undo and you run the risk of being blacklisted.

 

In reality, this spam killer criteria has one fundamental flaw: it’s based on your domain email server IP address. So you may wonder why it is such a problem if your email is hosted by Google Apps or sent by SendGrid. After all, it’s their IP that gets the blame.

 

Well for sure, but in order to protect THEIR own sender reputation, they will rely on similar mechanisms to determine your sender reputation, too. In the case of SendGrid, they will even kick you out if your sender reputation falls below 70 percent.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

What affects it?

Okay, so now that you know it’s important, you need to know what key factors will hurt your sender reputation the most so that you can take care of this.

 

There are five factors that will badly impact it:

 

1. High bounce rate

 

With emails, there are two sorts of bounces:

 

- Soft bounce, when the problem is temporary (e.g. inbox full)

- Hard bounce, when the address has been rejected by the destination server (the email doesn’t exist)

 

A high bounce rate strongly suggests the message is spam and will therefore impact your sender reputation.

 

You should go through significant effort to lower this in order to preserve your sender reputation (and ultimately your deliverability). So, just make sure that you are indeed emailing people with actual working emails.

 

This can be achieved by a combination of good prospecting (getting quality emails) and verification tools (we’ll cover this in the Prospecting section)

 

2. Spam complaints

 

When people label an email as “Spam” or “Junk,” not only will it reduce your ability to reach this user’s inbox, but all of those complaints will be aggregated to determine whether or not your email will land in other people’s inboxes, too.

 

Similar content is easily flagged as spam. So take advantage of personalization capabilities as much as you can to minimize this.

 

Note that emails like info@, marketing@, sales@, support@... and other generic emails usually generate more complaints than individual targeted emails like john.smith@company.com

 

3. Spam traps

 

This is a bad one.

 

Spam traps are fake (or obsolete) email addresses that are set by email providers and aren’t supposed to actually receive any emails.

 

Sending to these emails is a strong indicator that a list has been purchased and therefore a stronger likelihood that the sender is a spammer.

 

Currently, there are no tools to identify these obsolete email addresses, and even list cleaners remain clueless.

 

To minimize this, you need to make sure prospecting is done well.

 

4. Volume & consistency

 

Sending 500 emails in one day is another indicator that you are spamming. Instead, spread your usage over time so that you don’t send bursts of emails.

 

It’s best to send 100 emails a day, in one week, rather than sending 500 emails all in one day.

 

5. Too many recipients

 

This one is easy to avoid, as you’ll get the best results when you’re sending to just one person at a time.

 

Only send to between one and four people (not hundreds in one go).

 

 

 

 

 

 

Third parties

In order to boost their sender reputation, some people rely on email sending services (such as SendGrid or Mandrill) instead of Google Apps.

 

Companies like these developed special deals (feedback loop) with many of the email service providers to know when someone generates a complaint so that they can police their own users.

 

Note: It is still possible to deduce deliverability problems by monitoring closely your open rate. Sudden drop may indicate a deliverability problem.

 

Being able to monitor your sender reputation with a sending service is a fantastic feature, as you can closely monitor it to make sure your emails get delivered.

 

The flip-side of that is that you will end up in the promotional tab rather than the inbox, as services like Gmail will notice those emails come from sending companies (they know their IPs) and will be placed at the same level as email marketing sent by Aweber, MailChimp and all those other marketing tools. Prospects will also see a small “via mail59.atl51.rsgsv.net” next to your email address, which is not good to emulate personal reach out.

 

It’s still better than hitting the spam folder, but far from excellent for personal outreach emails that come from your personal email.

 

But although some tools allow you to white label emails being sent out so it looks genuine (with a small change in your DNS fields), your domain still run the risk of being blacklisted.

 

So, what do we do?

 

New domain

An easy solution nowadays is simply creating another domain name similar to that of your product, only completely dedicated to cold email. This way, you can easily discard it if you run into any problems.

 

For example, if you own a .com, you can buy the .co and do cold email from that. Another approach is to buy a similar or enriched name, like mydomainapp.com or getmydomain.com instead of mydomain.com.

 

Even massive companies like SendGrid have secondary domains to silo risks (E.g. they use sendgrid.net instead of sendgrid.com for link tracking)

 

Once the domain has been purchased, open a new Google Apps account and you’re ready to go (or buy the new domain directly with Google Apps for easier set-up). Plus, it’s easy enough to have your new domain redirect to your main domain if the user is curious enough to enter the email domain in the browser.

 

This approach sits very well with email service companies like Google (Google Apps), SendGrid, and many other email providers/senders (at least for now).

 

This would not have worked before the era of cloud emails when companies managed their own email infrastructure. This is because the IP wouldn’t have changed (so changing the domain name would not have changed the IP of the server sending the emails). Or this would have required new IPs to be warmed up first (sending a few simple emails to get recognized before sending higher volume)

 

With emails in the cloud, it became the responsibility of email service providers and email sending companies, so now it’s much easier to switch domains and reset your sender reputation.

 

Note that creating a new domain still requires some setup work. Make sure you get things right with SPF, DKIM and other counter spam mechanisms to get a high deliverability rate.

CHAPTER TWO

Boring but good to know

 

I am the law

OK, this is probably the most boring part of the whole book (at least for me), but you need to understand at least the bare minimum in order to know where you stand.

 

Fortunately, in most cases, there isn't much to know about it.

 

Of course, the law is different for each country.

 

 

Countries

 

USA

In the US, you need to know about CAN-SPAM (Controlling the Assault of Non-Solicited Pornography And Marketing). It’s an Act that was passed in 2003 for commercial emails. Yep, pornography and marketers are basically the same, at least legally speaking.

 

You can get all the information on the FTC page here:

 

CAN-SPAM Act: A Compliance Guide for Business - https://www.ftc.gov/tips-advice/business-center/guidance/can-spam-act-compliance-guide-business

 

But, since it’s boring as hell to read these, here is a short version with my take on it. If you’re still in doubt, always check with your business attorney. I’m not a layer and won’t be in court defending you based on my analysis of the text.

 

In short, you get fined up to $16,000 per email in violation of the CAN-SPAM act (with 2,000 emails per day for Google Apps accounts, the NSA could probably fund itself quickly while spying on emails). So either you conform to the legislation, or the solution you sell has got to be really expensive and your conversion rate awesome.

 

Let’s look at the first option in detail.

 

1. Don’t use false or misleading header information.

 

Sending emails pretending to be Elvis won’t fly here. It doesn’t state if nicknames/pen name work, and I have to wonder how actors using stage names would do it, but there you go. No more “Sandy from New York” when in fact it’s “Amor from the Philippines.”

 

2. Don’t use deceptive subject lines.

 

That will teach those spammers using “RE:” when they never sent a prior message (By the way, that’s bad practice. You don’t want to start a relationship based on tricking people.) Sure you may get great open rates, but you want positive replies.

 

3.Identify the message as an ad.

 

This one is tricky, because not all emails are ads. Fortunately, the law gives you a lot of leeway regarding how you go about this. Somehow I feel that “THIS IS AN AD” as a first line won’t get anyone very far.

 

4. Tell recipients where you’re located.

 

This one shouldn’t be too hard, assuming you use your business address.

 

5. Tell recipients how to opt out of receiving future email from you.

 

Now this one is interesting because it doesn’t have to be through a click as long as the instructions are clear. So technically speaking, “Please reply *I want more spam from you* to be removed from further communication” qualifies. Or to ask them to dance on one foot and send a picture of them doing it since that qualifies, too.

 

6. Honor opt-out requests promptly.

 

The law was passed in 2003 and administration at that time was using wooden PC, so “promptly” means within 10 business days in the context of the law.

 

Interesting fact: People have 30 days after you send them the email to opt-out (since the opt out system needs to be valid for at least 30 days after sending the email).

 

7. Monitor what others are doing on your behalf.

 

Basically, you can’t get away with it by hiring another company to do the dirty work for you.

 

That’s it.

 

The CAN-SPAM Act classifies email content in three categories:

1. Commercial content: messages that promote or advertise products or services.

2. Transactional or relationship content: messages that relate to existing business relationships or transactions.

3. Other content: neither a commercial nor a transactional relationship.

 

The problem is that “Other content” is vague and has no real examples.

 

The Act specifies: A commercial e-mail message is defined as any e-mail that has a “primary purpose of . . . commercial advertisement or promotion of a commercial product or service”

 

Let’s take the example of an inquiry email where you contact another business to find out who is in charge of marketing. Should an email like that still contain an unsubscribe mechanism? Its primary goal is to find the right person to talk to — not to advertise a product or service. It’s not considered “transactional” or “relationship” either, since you’ve never contacted the business before. So it seems like it falls in the “other” category. Gray area here…

 

And, because “unsubscribe” and such only have to appear when the *primary purpose* is commercial, we should be able to add a line toward the end of the message that says “Visit our website to check our exciting new products!” since it’s not the primary goal of this email.

 

So there you go. Messages asking, “Who is the marketing director?” while including a “Visit our website to check our exciting new products!” after your signature should not need an unsubscribe mechanism.

 

Of course, here is the disclaimer: I’m just an entrepreneur and I take calculated risks. You should not consider the contents of this article to be legal advice.

 

 

 

 

 

Canada

Canada has a much more constraining policy regarding commercial emails.

 

CASL (Canada’s anti-spam legislation) came into effect in July, 2014.

 

You can find the site here: http://fightspam.gc.ca/eic/site/030.nsf/eng/home

 

More rules were added in January 2015 highlighting the fact that it is now illegal to install programs, such as malware, on someone’s computer without content. Seriously? When was that ever legal?

 

And July 1, 2017, people will have an option to take civil action against company or individual that they believe has violated the law. Oh boy.

 

Here is the full text: http://laws-lois.justice.gc.ca/eng/acts/E-1.6/index.html

 

But if you are located *outside* Canada, you don’t need to care at all: “CASL allows Canadian enforcement against spammers operating in Canada.”

 

Or in plain text in the law: "12. (1) A person contravenes section 6 only if a computer system located in Canada is used to send or access the electronic message.” Interesting how absurd this is in our current interconnected world, isn’t it?

 

Note that users have 60 days to unsubscribe instead of 30 for the US. So the unsubscribe mechanism should be functional for at least 60 days.

 

On the bright side: “Penalties for the most serious violations of the Act can go as high as $1 million for individuals and $10 million for businesses. Civil cases cannot be brought before the courts until July 2017”. So make sure you collect more than $10 million before you get caught if you plan on breaking the Canadian law.

 

2017 may be a good year for lawyers in Canada, while spammers outside Canada won’t care at all. Strange world to penalize your own country.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Other countries

Each country has their own law. In the UK for example, you can cold email other businesses, but not individuals. So far so good, until you discover that solo contractors are considered individuals.

 

Given the economical situation of the world today, there are far more things to worry about than just spam, but it’s clear that more regulation will come.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

CHAPTER THREE

Your worst enemy

 

Yourself

To master the game, you need to know one enemy very well: yourself.

 

Below, you will find some really important concepts you need to keep in mind when doing cold emails.

 

Know your goals

Managing expectations is a big part of successful cold emails as a continuous growth channel.

 

I get it. You want to start cold emailing right now — but spending a bit of time reading about the mindset will help ensure you will have a better experience overall, which will help you reach your goals faster and stick with it longer.

 

Cold email can be used in a variety of ways, so it’s important to know why you are in the game.

 

Is it to obtain funding meetings? To promote a product or service? To book demos? To interview people? To get feedback? To land job interviews? To get the email addresses of decision-makers? To grow your LinkedIn connections? To get more attendees for your webinar?

 

Once you know your goal, you need to decide how you are going to track success and what success means to you. Does your idea of success mean that you want 10 percent of prospects to reply to you? Or 5 percent to book a demo with you? Or 20 percent to subscribe to your YouTube channel?

 

When you have a clear goal in mind, it’ll make the journey way easier. Do spend a bit of time on this — it’s not that hard, and it will help you become more successful according to your own definition of success.

 

Finally, cold email are infamous in B2C (Business to Consumer), you want to use it for B2B (Business to Business) instead. It doesn’t mean that your product should strictly be B2B though. For example, you may sell physical products to consumers but instead of emailing potential buyers, you could use cold emails to reach out to influencers (press, bloggers…) or retailers.

 

 

 

The 20 mile march

The next most important thing (after you know what you want) is allowing yourself enough time to achieve that goal.

 

If I told you that I wanted to be the best tennis player in the world, then gave myself two weeks to reach this goal, you’d laugh. It’s equally as strange to see people giving up after one month of cold emailing.

 

What’s really important with cold email is having a repeatable process.

 

You’ll be way more successful if you focus on the activity more than the results.

 

Let me give you an example. When I launched my business, my action was to contact 20 new businesses by email each day. The goal was not to obtain five demo calls because that was outside of my control. It was ultimately up to the prospect to book a demo with me, but contacting 20 new people each day was completely within my control. I could hold myself accountable.

 

If the results don’t match the actions, you can always change the actions.

 

In the previous example of becoming a tennis player, a bad measurement would be your global ranking (as it depends on who you meet and how good others are). A better measurement would be to train 10 hours each day. You can track that and be easily held accountable. Becoming number one is much more likely.

 

So if you want to be good at cold email, you’ve got to be prepared to contact a lot of people in the process!

 

Strategy vs Tactics

When it comes to writing high-performing emails, you should not study the tactics — instead, look at the underlying strategies.

 

If you study the tactics, you will be limited by what you learn. But if you study the strategies and the mindset, you will be able to generate an infinite number of great templates.

 

To start with, your goal should be to warm people up as fast as you can and build trust — NOT to close them on first email.

 

The money is almost never in the first email, so you have to think about relationship building instead.

 

To give you some perspective, a stranger in the street is actually easier to engage with than someone reading your email. Someone reading your email can simply hit delete and decide to ignore you. It's harder to deal with a physical version of someone right in front of you. Interestingly enough, people think it's tough to sell on the street. But in reality, it’s hard to sell anything before you establish a relationship. Not to mention that people on the street are probably not the right audience for you, and pre-qualifying people is a big part of success.

 

Asking for money in the first email (before establishing a relationship) is like a beggar on the street. You should aim to start a conversation first. On the street, that means asking for time or direction first… not money.

 

Shifting your mind from selling to being curious will not only increase your chances of selling, but it will also help you learn more about what makes a better product and the typical objections — which, in turn, will help you sell more.

 

Your mindset is very important — not just for writing good content, but for writing EFFECTIVE content.

 

Study copywriting masters and you’ll be unstoppable.

 

Dealing with rejections

One of the most difficult aspects of cold emailing is the rejection. And if you run a typical campaign, you will get rejected A LOT. Get used to it — this is not such a bad thing. What is much worse is actually not getting any responses and therefore not knowing where the other person stands.

 

If you follow the advice in this book, you should expect a response rate of between 20 percent and 30 percent with no effort. But out of those responses, a certain percentage will be rejections such as:

 

“No”, “no thank you,” “unsubscribe,” “get me off your list,” “this is not of interest to us,” “No interest. Thanks,” “Not interested,” “Sorry, we don't need these kinds of services at this time,” and so on.

 

You will even get the occasional person who’s having a really bad day and needs to get their frustration out:

 

“Stop spamming me. You're emailing me about a product that no longer exists. You're obviously not doing any actual research on which people you should be cold emailing and I certainly wouldn't use your services based on this exchange.”

 

“One more email from you and I'll take out a restraining order on you and your company”

 

“I get dozens of cold emails (i.e. spam) a day. It would be a full time job to reply to them all, and it is obviously made worse when the same people email me until they get a response. No offense intended, but if the first 3 emails didn't evoke a positive response, it's highly unlikely the 4th will.”

 

“I suggest you talk to these people: http://www.samaritans.org.nz Spamming me to get my attention GUARANTEES I won’t listen to you.”

 

These are all real answers by the way and on top of this, you need to add the hard and soft bounces when emails are invalids, mailbox full… and you’ll face a lot negativity and many just give up after “testing” cold emailing and wrongly assuming this is a channel that would not work for their business.

 

So, is it really worth it? How can you deal with all that negativity?

 

1. To start with, you have to recognize that this is part of the game. Not everyone likes the same things, so by default, you will get people who dislike what you have to say or offer. That’s OK. Your approach and the market you are in will generate very different reactions. Try cold emailing doctors with a friend-tone, dropping their MD title and you’ll see what I mean by that. Little things are big things with people.

 

2. It gets a lot easier when you have success to counter balance this.

 

Consider some of the other messages I’ve received from cold emails:

 

“Nice email. My co-founder Tom, would like to meet with you. I assume he can go via the calendar link?”

 

After my fourth email: “I appreciate you following up so many times. I have been busy, indeed. My inbox is a mess right now. I didn't forget about you. I simply planned to get back to you later. I have other priorities I need to deal with, but I promise to evaluate the applicability of your solution in the near future.”

 

“Hi Jeremy, well I am not so sure about rockstar, but happy to answer any questions if you are looking for user input on ideas.”

 

“I hope that you had a wonderful Christmas and are enjoying this special time of the year. That's an interesting question (…)”

 

“Hi Jeremy. Can we make it the following week? I'm on leave until then and am trying (for once) not to have any work meetings during that time.”

 

“Hi Jeremy. Sounds interesting, does Tuesday work?”

 

These are all very nice leads to get. So concentrate on those and track your ratio of rejection vs connections and aim for 1/1 to start with.

 

It’s been shown that being analytical makes us less emotional. So take advantage of this and start tracking numbers with your emails.

 

3. Another way is to rely heavily on automation. This means that you can set an entire week of automation and let it run without having to push the button to send email daily.

 

You can also not look at your cold email inbox for a few days to give yourself more of a chance of getting some positive responses too. The idea is to outsource all the emotional burden of sending emails and follow-ups to a cold-hearted machine that will relentlessly send emails whether you feel poorly or great, whether you’re sleeping, in a meeting, or just doing something else.

 

 

 

 

 

Expected results

Here’s how to measure your cold email efforts, so that you can get a good sense of how you’re doing.

 

1. Open rate - A prospect who opens a sequence — AKA at least one of the emails you send them.

 

80-100 percent: You are doing a great job

70-80 percent: The zone you should be in

60-70 percent: Low results

Under 60 percent: Rubbish

 

2. Click rate - Not easy to really quantify because the call to action with cold emails is rarely to have people click on links, but rather to reply to you. Here are some directions on “casual” links (a link to your company website, a link to your LinkedIn profile and so on).

 

30 percent and above: Doing fantastic

20-30 percent: Great results

10-20 percent: The zone you should be in

5-10 percent: Low results

Under 5 percent: Rubbish

 

3. Reply rate - People actually hitting reply and sending an email (this includes both positive and negative responses)

 

75-100 percent: You are doing amazingly, keep doing it, I can’t even get my family to reply in this range

50-75 percent: You are doing fantastic

40-50 percent: You are doing great

20-40 percent: The zone you should be in

10-20 percent: Low results

5-10 percent: Poor results, something needs to change.

Under 5 percent: Rubbish, you need to seriously review what’s going on.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

CHAPTER FOUR

How to get prospects who will reply?

 

Audience

Prospecting is the art of building a list of email addresses of people to contact.

 

Many businesses are so eager to start sending emails that they often neglect this step and buy existing lists.

 

Yet, this step is where A players spend most of their time. A great execution here is the strongest determining factor of success. The battle is often won or lost based on the quality of your prospect list.

 

If you think this is too strong of a statement, let me give you some perspective.

 

I was visiting a castle in Portugal not long ago. It was packed with tourists and pedestrian traffic was strong. Sounds like the perfect opportunity to sell my Internet service since I will never run out of prospects to talk to, right?

 

Well, of course not. The audience was wrongly targeted and the context for doing business was bad. People in the castle were just not the type of people interested in a cold emailing service (not to mention all the tourists whose language I didn’t speak). And even if they could have been buyers, they were enjoying their holidays and it would not have been appropriate to approach them about any type of business deal. I would have wasted both my time and theirs, and the results would have been terrible.

 

Even the best product and great copywriting skills won't get you far if you’re talking to the wrong audience.

 

On the other hand, imagine if I had been at a conference where cold email was the main focus. Prospects would be pre-qualified to be potential clients and the context would be very favorable to conduct business.

 

Quantity never beats quality. When you do manage to get the quality you need, you can look at how to increase quantity -- not the opposite.

 

Neglecting prospecting will create a lot of negativity and endanger your sender reputation. Companies may even decide to give up on this channel due to poor execution.

 

So let’s look at how emails can be gathered, along with how to target prospects who will be interested in your solution and willing to listen.

 

 

Strategy

Your strategy to acquire new prospects will vary drastically depending on your business being B2C (selling to consumers, private individuals) or B2B (selling to other businesses).

 

On top of this, your industry will play a major role, too.

 

For example, if you’re a brick-and-mortar business, you may have success with Yelp, Googling “profession + town,” or even using Google Maps. Those techniques are not really right for high-tech companies, though.

 

One way to figure out how to find your core audience is to look at all your clients/users and find patterns. It could be the company size, the number of products they sell, platform they use, or their web traffic. For B2C, it could be hobbies/passions, sites they visit regularly, or books they read. After that, figure out where people fitting your criteria hang out.

 

Think broadly. You can expand your research by looking at clients of the companies you have as clients or even clients of your own competition.

 

Here you’ll find a lot of ways to find new prospects, pick the ones that seem the most promising, and give it a go.

 

No matter what technique you use to acquire new prospects, make sure you collect real people’s emails, not groups (like sales@… or info@…) unless you want to ask around for the right person to contact. But be aware that group emails generally lead to poor results and more spam complaints.

 

Buying lists

Okay, this one is simple. Never buy a list. Just don’t. You should stay far away. Seriously. I mean it.

 

In this situation, you are dealing with both high quantity AND low quality. Exactly what I said you should not do. Poor quality will harm you more than it will help you (e.g. your sender reputation will suffer).

 

Lists produce a significant amount of outdated contacts, so if emails don’t bounce as invalid, it’s likely that they won’t be read at all or that the person you contacted is no longer responsible for the same things anymore.

 

Even worse than that? Lists can contain spam traps (emails setup by providers that no one is supposed to actually email). If you do happen to email these spam traps, you will quickly be flagged as a spammer. Also, there is no way to figure out if the email is a spam trap or not (even email verification services won’t be able to tell you).

 

There may be exceptions, but 99.9 percent of the time, it’s just not worth it.

 

Databases

Databases are similar to list buying, except you make your own list based on data present in the system.

 

Those databases, unless refreshed regularly, go stale pretty quickly and you end up with old contacts (I’ve seen databases with contact details as many as five years old) or misleading company information (e.g. Google mentioned as a medium size company with 100 employees, which was true at some point in the past).

 

The incentive to clean up the data for those services is pretty low (since they tend to advertise themselves based on number of contacts), so if you decide to pursue this approach because tapping into the database means you can have a filter matching your target audience, you would need to use a service to verify the validity of those emails at the very least.

 

Some examples of sites that fall into this category include Data.com, Leadferret.com ( horrible personal experience ), and Hoovers.com

 

But unless you can’t figure out how to gather emails any other way (which in most cases is not that hard to figure out), I would avoid those services like the plague.

 

Scraping

Scraping (reading data from Internet pages in an automated way) is similar to buying a list except you are doing it yourself and therefore impact of outdated/wrong data is greatly minimized because you know when you collected the information. That said, you will still be relying on when the data was published in the first place and how often it’s updated.

 

This technique applies very well to associations or directories related to your target market. It can literally be a goldmine for your business.

 

You’ll need to be slightly careful with it, though, as it’s a bit of a gray area regarding permission to do so. But it could be worth the effort to check, as it’s one of the fastest ways to get a lot of highly targeted prospects for your market or niche.

 

The challenge with Internet data is the lack of uniformity of the data, so you’ll need a custom script specifically designed for extracting the data you need (don’t look for a “download as .csv” button, you’re unlikely to find one). Those small scripts are not very hard to build, but may prove challenging for non-technical people.

 

Here are a few tools that could easily help you out with this if you don’t want to get technical:

 

- Import.io & kimonolabs.com: These two sites aim at providing you with a tool to design what you want to extract.

 

- Fiverr.com : Just pay someone to do it for you.

 

- Upwork.com : For heavier work, you can check out contractors willing to embark in a small project.

 

Good examples of this technique would be the association of financial planners in Country X, early-stage/newly funded companies on AngelList, or a directory of realtors in region X on a house renting site.

 

 

Tech Websites

Somewhat similar to scraping, you may want to target a particular niche of prospects using a certain technology which can be determined by visiting a prospect’s website.

 

For example, e-commerce websites often rely on existing technology (e.g. Magento) instead of building their own sites from scratch. So, let’s say your company happens to provide an extension for Magento. It’d be incredibly valuable to figure out which websites are using Magento, wouldn’t it? That’d be a smart way to pre-qualify companies to talk to (rather than simply targeting all e-commerce sites).

 

Some solutions offer exactly that — they search the Internet to see which websites use a certain technology and then sell the lists.

 

The challenge is reaching the right person in the organization. Having a lot of info@, sales@,… will usually yield poor results, so you’ll need to do more research and find the right person to talk to for each and every organization.

 

Alternatively, you could do “multiple passes.” In the first email, you would ask for the appropriate person to talk to about your product, and the second email would be about engaging with that person.

 

Some examples of websites providing such lists:

 

BuiltWith.com , Wappalyzer.com , and Datanyze.com

 

 

 

 

LinkedIn

LinkedIn can be a strong channel for finding new prospects for many reasons — the first being that users are generally updating their profiles as their situations change. So, you end up with relatively good and up-to-date information.

 

Another reason is that it elegantly bypasses the spam legislation. This is because people regularly receive email from LinkedIn, as they agreed to receive this communication.

 

But it’s also a great way to find people based on company and job title. The only problem is that you don’t get email addresses until you are connected.

 

Sending personalized invitations does work to some extent, but it may backfire if too many people mention they don’t know you (your account runs the risk of being restricted). It happens so often that LinkedIn has a self service when this happens the first time. What they don’t tell you is that your existing invites are NOT removed, so if people keep marking you as unknown, it will count toward your second strike, so be careful there and ask them to remove ll pending invitation if that happens to you.

 

Another technique is to join groups your prospects belong to and simply send them a message. This can work like a charm. Just remember that email best practices apply here too.

 

With LinkedIn searches, you can easily build a highly targeted and accurate list of prospects to contact with minimum intellectual effort. For example: “Founder of a SaaS company,” “Financial planner in a company of less than five employees,” and so on.

 

If all else fails, you can still get people’s email addresses with a few tools. For example, prospecthive.com or SalesLoft.com. Plus, you can check out the resources at the end of this chapter for more tools and ideas to obtain emails.

 

Once you figure out the email address of your prospect, you can use an interesting tactic by mentioning shared connections. E.g. “I noticed we are both connected to {{common_connections}}. Small world.”

 

If you think all of this is too much effort, create a step-by-step process to follow and get a virtual assistant to do it for you. Works like a charm.

 

Another benefit of using LinkedIn is that it forces you to clearly define your target audience in measurable terms (e.g. size of the company, position in the company...), which will be greatly beneficial as opposed to buying a list and hoping that some people in the list would be interested.

 

Brick and mortar

When contacting brick and mortar companies, Yelp ( http://www.yelp.com/ ) or other local directories can be of great help. The process would be to determine the company and then figure out the right person to contact in that company (similar to getting a list of tech websites).

 

Another approach might be to do a quick Google search and localize the results by adding the town, E.g. “Hairdresser Chicago.” Then you could either look for the hairdressers that come up in the search results or go Google Maps and look around for the companies you want to target.

 

 

Then you could ask your virtual assistant to go through the process and generate a list of prospects by finding targeted contacts through the website results.

 

 

Inbound

“Inbound” means making it easier for people to find you, be it through advertisement, guest posting, social media, marketing, etc.

 

This produces one of the best sources of prospects, since they pre-qualify themselves as potentially interested. They won’t be as cold as totally unknown prospects and this leads to better results when reaching out to them.

 

To use the analogy of speaking to a stranger on the street (a cold prospect), imagine that they’ve already entered your store. You know they are the right audience, or that at least they’d be more receptive to what you have to say.

 

A good example would be to reach out to someone who has expressed interest in an eBook on your site (a lead magnet), someone who signed up for your newsletter, someone who attended your webinar, or someone who commented on your blog.

 

Capture their email and then reach out to them. This leads to a great reply rate and many positive responses.

 

 

Conferences

Conferences can be a big win for you if the audience is in tune with your solution, and if you’re genuinely providing value.

 

The downside, of course, is that you will need to be there physically (which could cost you a fortune). It also requires a different set of skills to be able to engage with people in person.

 

Although prospects may be cold at a conference, the big advantage is that people are in the mindset of discovering and being entertained. Most are getting paid to attend the conference by their employer without any obligatory results (most of the time). As for owners, most would be willing to network and/or bring back something from the conference, be it leads for the business or potential solutions to their problems. In short, they will be more receptive to getting pitched.

 

Speaking engagements are brilliant tactics for conferences, as being a speaker gives you a tremendous amount of exposure and you’ll be able to make connections easier.

 

If you are an exhibitor, sometimes you will have access to a list of participants. This can be pure gold. You can pre-warm them before the conference (by email or targeted ads), then contact them again after the conference.

 

But conferences can also be useful even when you’re not attending. They make great excuses to reach out:

 

“Hey Jeremy, are you going to the super kick-ass conference next Tuesday? I can’t make it but wanted to get in touch with you…”

 

Referrals

Referrals are one of the most powerful lead generators you can find, but they are also one of the hardest to scale.

 

How about asking your existing customers/users/connections for prospects? Depending on your service/approach, you can link this with the LinkedIn tactic described earlier (common connections).

 

One cool tip is to scan out-of-office messages, as they will often include the contact details of another person in charge. Use this and start another email sequence for these new contacts. “Hey, I noticed that {{previous_contact}} is out of the office, I wanted to…”

 

Outsource team

Finally, if generating a list of prospects/leads is too painful, you can always hire someone else to do it for you.

 

Companies such as LeadGenius.com specialize in exactly this. So, if you have some money and don’t mind outsourcing how prospects are contacted, this may be a good alternative for your business.

 

Getting their email!

One important skill when it comes to prospecting is the ability to find emails based on name and domain.

 

For example, if you know there is a Jeremy Chatelaine working at Quickmail.io, it’d be nice if you could use a tool to easily find an email address.

 

Here are a few ways to get emails:

 

- Datanyze Insider is free and works quite nicely.

 

- SellHack.com allows for 10 free checks per month.

 

- Getsidekick.com allows you to grab emails from the websites you visit.

 

- SnapBird.org allows you to search people’s Twitter timelines, so you can check if they’ve ever shared their email with their followers. You can also use the Advanced Twitter Search.

 

- Toofr.com allows you to find email from domain and name

 

- By law, a website’s Terms and Conditions have to contain a valid email address.

 

- Google. Sometimes it just works to search for the name of someone and add “email” to your search query.

 

- One more try with VoilaNorbert.com and EmailHunter.co

 

- Last resort: Use http://mailtester.com/ and guess the name. Most of the time, it’s not that hard to find, but if you need a few more ideas for permutations because default tools are failing, you can always try this spreadsheet (make a copy and edit the yellow fields): http://bit.ly/1n4imPF

 

 

 

Verify them

Depending on how you generate prospects, getting new email addresses will generate more or less bounces (invalid email addresses), which in turn will spam your mailbox with useless bounced messages which will affect your sender reputation and your deliverability rate.

 

To preserve your sender reputation, it’s always wise to verify emails first. This is especially true when there is any suspicion as to the validity of the emails in question.

 

It's important to understand how most of these verifications work so that you know the risks and can make better judgment calls.

 

Most (if not all) of those tools will use an exploit in the SMTP protocol (sending an email to another server). They will basically start communicating with the email server responsible for the domain (e.g. domain.com) and ask if it’s okay to send a message to MisterX@domain.com. What happens next depends on the configuration of the server.

 

1. The server behaves as expected and will respond “Sure, please proceed” if the email exists -- or “Sorry, I don’t know any MisterX.” At that point, the connection is dropped and the tool knows whether MisterX exists or not.

 

2. The server is set to allow-all and will tell you to “proceed” with your request. The validation of an email will only happen after the email has actually been sent. At this stage, you won’t know whether the email exists or not until you send the email. So the tool won’t be able to tell you if MisterX exists — it’ll only tell you that you can send emails to MisterX@domain.com and that you’ll run the risk of being rejected.

 

3. Finally, some servers are too slow and simply don’t reply fast enough. This sometimes leads to soft bounces (Warning: this email is delayed), or the SMTP server is just freaking slow so the tool gives up. Best not to contact those ones.

 

Here are two tools that people use to verify emails in bulk: KickBox.io and BriteVerify.com

 

It’s important to note that spam traps (fake email addresses setup by providers to flag people as spammers) will not be detected by any services, as email providers don’t advertise them.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

PART II

The secret sauce to get replies on demand

 

PART II: The secret sauce to getting replies on demand

 

Backed by facts

Getting replies is not that complicated if you follow a few simple guidelines.

 

What I’m about to tell you is backed by facts (data pulled from millions of automated emails).

 

What’s more is that I looked at what works and what doesn’t work to determine what really makes a difference (while killing some urban myths in the process).

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

CHAPTER FIVE

Worse / Best practices

 

Wrong audience

This is by far the best kept secret of people who share their success publicly on the Internet. The people who write these articles will talk about how well-crafted their email was, how attention-grabbing their subject line was, and so on. Not once will you hear about how well-aligned the audience was with the message in the first place.

 

Oh, for sure message crafting is a very important part, but the truth is that who you try to contact will make or break your campaign. Having a poor list is by far the number one reason why people fail at cold email. Plus, emailing bad prospects can have serious consequences in terms of future deliverability.

 

The good news is that it’s not that hard to address this. You need to pay more attention to the prospecting phase and find the best quality prospects you possibly can.

 

The reason why most people skip this crucial step is because it’s not always easy to figure out who you should reach out to and where to find them.

 

For a startup trying to figure out their market or test a new vertical to expand their product, this can be challenging. For more established businesses, they likely already have a persona to go after, so it’s just a matter of researching a bit more.

 

If that’s a problem for you, try to limit yourself to 100 emails per source to verify how well prospects from different sources respond to your offering. Then try another way of acquiring prospects (as mentioned in the previous section of this book).

 

 

 

 

 

Subject line

It’s amusing to see how much emphasis has been placed on subject lines recently.

 

The promise is that if people don’t open your email, they won’t reply. For sure, but what data tells me is that subject lines act more as filters to prevent reading rather than a priority list.

 

What I mean by that is that people don’t seem to be that selective about the emails they read. They do delete emails with subjects that appear to have failed their personal filter, though. For example, mentioning “Make money fast while working at home” won’t help your open rate in a B2B setup.

 

Remember that a subject line is a contract between the reader and the writer as to what is expected in the body of the email.

 

So whatever you chose, make sure that the title and body are aligned. A subject line that says X but delivers Y in the body will do you no good. You may get a great open rate, but your positive reply rate will be bad.

 

Artificially increasing open rate is easy, just use this: “Your child had an accident this morning”.

 

Please don’t start the first email with “RE:” and strive for consistency. This promotes trust, which is always good for business. Why would you ever want to start a new relationship on a false promise?

 

A question I often get is “Should I personalize the subject line with someone’s first name?” Data shows that it doesn’t matter. I’ve sees very high open rates and very low open rates with the same subject lines.

 

“Hi {{firstname}} - Strange question?” - 40 percent open rate (leading to a 4.5 percent reply rate)

“Hi {{firstname}} - Strange question?” - 80 percent open rate (leading to a 40.17 percent reply rate)

 

If you don’t like Gmail stacking threads of all the cold emails you send, having a personalized subject line may help.

 

So stop stressing over subject lines. Instead, only contact people who would be interested in your offering. The quality of prospects matters more.

 

Here are a few subject lines you can use:

 

“Quick question” - A simple one, but make sure the body of your email is short. People will like your consistency.

 

“{{Referred By}} referred me” - Name drop, baby. Works great.

 

“Looking for Senior Move Managers” - See how it doesn’t matter as long as it’s targeted?

 

“Coffee maybe?”, “Meeting today?” - We are in the business of relationships. This is a nice way to express it. Make sure the body is aligned (e.g. you do actually ask for a meeting of some sort).

 

“Strange question about your blog comment”, “Who is the contact at your company for Sales?”, “Can you help?”…

 

 

 

Talking to many

This is the typical newsletter or mailing list approach that doesn’t speak to a single individual, but to a group.

 

It might have pretty HTML fonts, nice pictures, borders, and so on.

 

If your goal is to get replies, you may want to think again.

 

This type of email doesn’t go very far in terms of reply rate. They usually perform at 2 or 3 percent reply rate (with follow ups!).

 

It may look nice, but it screams that you don’t intend on speaking to them specifically (or anyone in particular). And speaking to everyone is like speaking to no one.

 

I’ve seen people rely on MailChimp for cold email and then wonder why nobody replies to them. Whatever system you use for cold email needs to come from your own mailbox (be it work or personal) or people will notice instantly and disengage. Marketing automation sucks when it comes to cold email.

 

You have to imagine you are writing to one person. When you do so, it becomes obvious that you would not use a pretty HTML email for just one person.

 

Cold email is not a beauty contest. Stay simple.

 

Salesy

A salesy email is like a doctor giving you a pill before examining you. It’s hard to trust.

 

Sales emails use all sorts of rubbish tactics including scarcity and urgency (not good for building relationships, by the way). On top of that, they promote buyer’s remorse, which is terrible for future sales through referrals.

 

You can still do it intelligently, but 90 percent of people do it incorrectly.

 

People don’t like to be forced into entering a deal and will resent it. You’d be lucky to get a reply rate of more than 3 percent with this approach.

 

Understand that money is never in the first email. Allow yourself more touches to close the sale. The first touch should be to pre-qualify, get a name to follow up with, and then reach out to engage in conversation.

 

Fortunately for us, there are better strategies.

 

Confusion

One big problem with not knowing your audience is that it usually operates in tandem with not knowing how to position your value proposition. Yet, it should be crystal clear.

 

Consider this email:

 

“Hi Jeremy,

 

It was nice to meet you again at the Geek on a Farm conference. It was fantastic, as usual.

 

We’re contacting you today because we’ve developed a multifunctional system to link the supplier and e-shops. I know that you were interested in sales increasing.

If you want to improve your connection flows with e-shops and, therefore, increase your sales, we can help you with a multifunctional free-to-use system. The main idea is to provide the data-cleaning services, so you can speak the same data-language with your clients. Also, the system permits atomize flows to make it easy and clear.

 

The system will be connected to your stock, and automatically sends the information to the vitrine of an e-shop. You can easily configure all details and special sales conditions. You can also integrate the e-shops you already working with.

 

Well, if you want a demo, or you have any questions, don't hesitate to contact me! Within only 30 minutes, you can get a key to strengthen your business and to increase you sales up to 30 percent.

 

Here is the system if you want to check it out: (website)

 

Have a nice day,

 

Looking forward to hearing from you and to meeting you again,

 

John”

 

At this point, I have no freaking idea what the value proposition is. How will entering a relationship with this person benefit me? And I’m not even sure what to do next. So, this email will likely get deleted or it will be forgotten.

 

Now consider this email:

 

“Hi Jeremy,

 

It was nice to meet you again at the Geek on a Farm conference. It was fantastic, as usual.

 

I’m contacting you today because we just developed a system to better link suppliers and e-shops, and I was wondering if that could be something of interest to you.

We take care of language translation so that you can integrate with e-shops worldwide. Our customers easily increase their sales by 30 percent within just three months of the integration.

 

Would you be free for a 20-minute personal demo next Tuesday? If not, how does your calendar look for next week?

 

Be amazing,

John”

 

I know roughly what problem it solves now (multi-language support) and the benefit (increasing international sales) and what to do next (check my calendar for next Tuesday).

 

Don’t try to confuse the reader with lots of information about your product. More is less when it comes to communication.

 

Be clear, to-the-point, and respectful of the reader’s time.

 

The call to action needs to be super clear.

 

 

Low commitment

When you need a higher response rate, lower the commitment.

 

In essence, ask your readers to do something so easy that they can’t refuse.

 

Examples of this approach:

 

“Reply *yes* and I’ll send you more information” - No need to type back three pages, just *yes*.

 

“Who is the appropriate person to speak to about X” - This is a great one because it’s easy to reply that it’s John who is looking after that. This works well with follow-ups because the annoyance of getting more emails can easily be solved by just replying with something short that they already know: someone’s name.

 

Another tactic is to add links and ask them to click on the correct one. Then, once they click, you can engage more deeply with them. Low commitments will allow for bigger ones later. “Hey, thanks for letting me know. I have another question I wanted to ask…” Could be a great follow-up to the prospect clicking a link.

 

Find ways to have your user not do too much work. Remember that you are still a stranger to him at this point.

 

Likeness

If you want to play with the pros, the best thing you can do is find commonalities between you and your prospects.

 

Consider this email, for example:

 

“I am a student in an executive entrepreneurship program and I am researching the typical challenges video game developers face in their day-to-day business. With a master’s degree in gaming, I'm passionate about helping video game developers.”

 

If you are a video game developer and receive this email, this will resonate strongly with you. You will feel like it was a version of you a few years back and would naturally want to help.

 

The world unfortunately has the tendency to see things as *us* vs *them*, so you need to find a way to be in the *us* category. You will then receive premium attention and special treatment.

 

It could be that you come from the same place, have the same hobbies, know the same people, have the same tastes… whatever.

 

I used to contact other business founders, and being one myself helped tremendously to connect. Add to that the promise to share a funny story about how I started my business and you have people suddenly thinking about how they started their business, which was often a struggle. That’s playing in the *us* zone, and that means replies -- positive replies -- and lots of them.

 

 

 

 

 

 

Reciprocity

Reciprocity is a powerful vector of goodwill. In short, it relies on giving without expecting anything in return. People will then feel the desire to reciprocate the favor one way or another in bigger terms.

 

To harness this, you need to find out what is of value to your prospects (not to you) and give it to them.

 

It could be a free eBook, a free report made specifically for them, a special video (only when they reply with interest, of course, or it won’t be scalable), or a piece of information they might find valuable.

 

And sometimes, it could be as simple as a personal demo of your product.

 

“No string attached, this is my gift to you on this beautiful Wednesday,” will certainly help generate good will. By your next email, you should not be cold anymore. Now you suddenly have something to talk about.

 

Links

Not sure where this one came from, but I often see people with tons of links in the body of their emails.

 

Know that too many links will hurt your deliverability, and each link is an opportunity for the reader to get distracted and forget about your email as he/she opens up another tab.

 

If you insist on using links, a good tactic is to place just one at the end of your email to track whether or not your prospect reads to the end. It’s a good indicator of interest, and cunning salespeople are quick to call the prospect while he/she is still in that mindset of checking their email.

 

Advanced users will link it with Google Analytics using utm_source, utm_campaign, utm_medium and utm_content so that the cold email campaign’s traffic will be visible in Google Analytics.

 

This is how you would structure your link:

 

http://www.example.com/?utm_campaign=june&utm_medium=email&utm_source=coldemail&utm_content=signature

 

There are a total of five parameters you can add to your URLs.

 

For example, you could set the parameter utm_source as coldemail to identify traffic that come from a your cold emails. You could then combine it with the parameter utm_campaign set to june to identify that this was the campaign in the month of June.

 

You should always use utm_source, utm_medium, and utm_campaign parameters to help keep track of different campaigns. utm_term and utm_content can be used for tracking additional information.

 

An easy way to obfuscate long links and force users to click on them to discover what they are is to use a shortening service like Bitly.com . The problem with such services is that they are referenced by URL spam lists and many emails will be rejected for spam content.

 

References:

 

http://www.invaluement.com/research/ : A spam list website many Spam filter use to know if your email contain link that are considered as spam.

 

http://mxtoolbox.com/blacklists.aspx : This quickly checks if a URL or domain is referenced in major spam lists.

 

 

Links as replies

You can also use links as substitutes for replies.

 

Consider this email:

 

“Hi {{firstname}},

 

I've attempted to reach you, but have had no success. Either alligators have eaten you or you're just plain swamped.

 

I put together this one-click survey to make it super easy for you, so no excuses for not answering me:

 

[link] An alligator ate me, please send flowers

[link] I wasn’t, but you may have wished I was because I’m not interested

[link] Hey, I must have missed your email

[link] Other

 

You rock!

[Your Name]

[Your Company]”

 

At this stage, you could capture results with Clickinsights.io , which is specially designed for this case. But if you don’t have money to spend on an experiment, each link can simply be a Bitly.com link leading to a different Google Form (or TypeForm.com , or Wufoo.com ). That way, you can track the number of clicks and get responses too.

 

Remember that small commitments lead to larger ones and it’s super easy to click on a link.

 

So it could have text like this:

 

“Thanks for letting me know! Could you write a few words below to give me some more details, pretty please?”

 

While they are here, they may as well enter a few words.

 

Then when they submit, redirect them to a page with a lead magnet, which then redirects them to a thank you page and a 30-minute demo to book… I’m kidding (only partly), but you get the idea.

 

 

 

Animated GIFs

Most, if not all, email readers have an option to prevent images from loading automatically, which prevents software relying on pictures being loaded to properly monitor open rates. But as humans, we remain curious.

 

So if you want your open rate to go through the roof (bypassing people reluctant to load images), you need to make sure people download the image beacon in the email. For that, there is nothing better than adding a picture in the text and referring to it. Because most (all?) email readers are booleans (either they load or they don’t load images). This means people will download all images to be able to see that picture. And this includes the image beacon that determines if someone opened the email or not.

 

Experiment with animated GIFs and simple pictures and see if you market is receptive to it.

 

- Trying to sell a product? Show a picture of your product and make a note about what is awesome in the picture.

 

- Sending a follow up? Add a funny animated GIF of someone waiting or being chased by an alligator…

 

You get the point.

 

 

 

Customization

Customization of the email body for each prospect is important. This isn’t just for getting replies (as you can still get a lot of replies with a good prospect list and a good message), but also to bypass bulk email spam filters so that you reach your prospect’s inbox.

 

If you send a lot of similar messages, services such as Gmail will pick up on this and automatically move the message to the spam folder, noting that many other people received a similar message.

 

A good way to scale this is to have a virtual assistant go over each prospect and write something about them for the first line or a PS at the end.

 

For example: “Loved your last blog article on X” or “PS: Felt the same about your tweet on X”

 

If this is too hard to get or to put in place, try to alternate different message variations and pace your emails to avoid similar message detection.

 

Pre-warming

Okay, this is pretty advanced stuff, but for those of you who get this, here is a nice way to pre-warm your prospects using ads:

 

Imagine you have your list of prospects. You can then import them as your target audience for a Facebook ad. Your prospects will be exposed to your brand via a well-crafted ad.

 

A few days after (whether they click on the ad), you start your cold email sequence (including a Facebook tracking pixel). People may then remember having seen your brand somewhere already and you can track success with that Facebook pixel.

 

If they visit your website, you obviously also have them in a re-targeting ad group ( https://www.adroll.com/getting-started/retargeting )

 

 

 

Summary

In summary, here you go, here is the magic formula to get responses:

 

1. Get your audience right. Find people who want to hear your offer.

 

2. Find ways to pre-warm the message (likeness works wonders).

 

3. Use simple subject lines.

 

4. Be short, to-the-point, and crystal clear as to what your value proposition is.

 

5. Make sure you talk to one person.

 

6. Ask for a low commitment call to action.

 

7. Follow-up relentlessly.

 

After that, a 40 percent reply rate should be easy to get.
